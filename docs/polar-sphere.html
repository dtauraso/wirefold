The Polar System for a Sphere

How 2D polar (r, θ) grows into 3D spherical (r, θ, φ) — and the parts that only exist in 3D.

Flat polar — two numbers

On a flat plane, a point is named by (r, θ):

  • r — distance from the center.
  • θ — the angle around the center, from a reference direction.

The point sits where the radius (length r, pointing at angle θ) lands on the circle. Two numbers — one length, one turn — name it completely.

Everything in 2D lives in this one plane: there is only one circle and one angle to turn through. To move the point you change r (out or in) or change θ (turn around the center) — nothing else.

Spherical — three numbers, two angles

On a sphere a point is (r, θ, φ):

  • r — distance from the center (on the surface, r = R). This is zoom.
  • φ — the polar angle: how far the radius tips down from the pole. 0 at the top pole, 90° at the equator, 180° at the bottom.
  • θ — how far around the pole the radius is turned, measured along the equator.

Direction to the point, then scale by R:

dir = cos φ·pole + sin φ·(cos θ·refX + sin θ·refY)

Two angles fully name a direction; the radius says how far out. That is the whole system.

Globe analogy: φ is latitude (from the pole), θ is longitude. Lines of constant θ are meridians; lines of constant φ are parallels; the equator is the parallel at φ = 90°.

1 · A second angle, out of the plane

2D only turns θ. 3D adds φ, which lifts the radius off the equator toward the pole — the motion the flat plane can't represent.

2 · The radius sweeps a disk, not a line

Spin a point around the pole and its radius traces a disk (a whole circle in 3D). Which disk depends on the latitude φ. In 2D there is only ever the one circle.

3 · Great circles, and where they cross

Meridians and the equator are great circles. Two distinct great circles always meet at two opposite points — the basis for the pan guide's intersection line. Nothing in 2D has this.

4 · The pole singularity

At a pole (φ = 0 or 180°) every longitude collapses to one point, so θ is undefined. It's the one place the coordinates break — a real point you can avoid, unlike the noisy blow-ups Cartesian cross products have.

The frame you fix first

Before any point has coordinates you pin a frame:

  • Center C — the origin.
  • pole — the chosen "up" axis (here the diagram's own top). It pierces the sphere at the two poles.
  • refX / refY — two perpendicular directions in the equatorial plane; θ is measured from refX.
  • equator — the great circle perpendicular to the pole.

That's the only place a Cartesian basis is built. After that everything is angle arithmetic about the pole: "spin" is θ + Δ, "tip" is φ + Δ, "perpendicular" is +90° — none of which can degenerate.

The pan triangle, in 3D

This is the 2D right triangle (x = r cos θ) lifted onto the sphere — the construction the editor's pan guide draws:

  • hypotenuse — the radius C → P to the cursor point (length R).
  • green line — the equator (horizontal torus) diameter at the cursor's turn θ.
  • foot G — the cursor's horizontal shadow, R·sin φ along θ, sitting on the green line.
  • the right angle is at G: C→G (the pan offset) is horizontal, G→P (= R·cos φ) runs straight up the pole — perpendicular by construction, every frame.

The 3D difference: in 2D the right angle is just a corner in the plane. Here the green line is the crossing of two great circles (disk ∩ equator), and the height rises out of the equatorial plane toward the pole. The same trig, but the legs now live on different circles of the sphere.

Because G is placed by the angle θ (always defined) and offset R·sin φ, the right angle can never "flip" — there is no cross product to collapse.

How Cartesian does pan

Pan in CAD/3D tools is a flat screen-space translation. The drag (Δx, Δy) in pixels becomes a world vector along the camera's own right/up axes:

The editor now pans polar (the "Mouse as polar" / "pan → recenter" tabs): the wheel (Δx, Δy) is converted to (r, angle) at the input edge, the slide runs in the refX–pole plane, an overlay shows the construction during the burst, and the polar origin recenters. Cartesian survives only at the (right, up) output edge — this tab is the contrast it replaced.

Δ = right·(Δx·s) + up·(−Δy·s)

where s = world-units-per-pixel at the look distance. Then both the camera and its target move by the same Δ:

  • every node shifts by the identical vector — the whole scene slides rigidly (left figure);
  • camera + target ride along together, so the look direction and zoom are untouched (right figure);
  • no center, no angle, no radius — it's pure addition of an (x,y,z) vector.

Why it's "Cartesian"

It only uses vector addition in the view plane. There's no pole, no θ/φ, nothing rotates. It's the one navigation op that stays flat even in the orbit-camera tools.

Contrast with polar pan: the polar version doesn't translate a camera through space. It rebases the sphere's origin by the triangle's base offset and recomputes every node's (r,θ,φ) about the new center — an operation on the model, in angles, not a Cartesian slide of the view.

The mouse as its own polar sphere

What the input provides: the browser reports every gesture in Cartesian — a wheel/trackpad WheelEvent hands you (deltaX, deltaY) in pixels (a PointerEvent hands you clientX, clientY). There is no polar event; this is the irreducible input edge, the mirror of the toWorld edge that feeds three.js. It is reinterpreted as polar at the first step — r = hypot(Δx, Δy), angle = atan2(Δy, Δx) from refX — and nothing Cartesian flows past it.

Cartesian pan reads the drag as (Δx, Δy) — two independent linear screen distances, added straight into a world translation.

Now assume the mouse makes its own polar sphere, in the conventional frame:

  • pole = up.
  • refX = horizontal; r (= Δx) lies along it to the surface.
  • the 3rd line (refY), ⊥ to both pole and refX, is the view — it points into the screen, the direction you look along.

So the drag lives in the plane facing you — the refX–pole plane (horizontal × up = the screen). The pan is a slide there by r at angle: Δx along refX, Δy along the pole. The 3rd line (the view) is never moved — you slide across the face of the sphere, perpendicular to your view.

The frame is fixed by the one direction map — the 3rd line is the quarter-turn of refX about the pole, in angles only:

pole : φ = 0  ·  refX : φ = 90°, θ = 0  ·  line3 (view) : φ = 90°, θ = 90°

d(θ,φ) = cos φ · pole + sin φ · (cos θ · refX + sin θ · line3), with pole = d(θ,0), refX = d(0,90°), line3 = d(90°,90°) — ⊥ to both by the angle alone, no cross product.

Pan is a TRANSLATION — the same action Cartesian pan does. The origin slides in a straight line in the refX–pole plane (the screen) by r at angle — Δx along refX, Δy along the pole. Nothing rotates; the view (the 3rd line) holds still.

So the input itself is polar — (Δx, Δy) arrives as (r, angle) and the slide stays a clean linear pan in the screen plane — no Cartesian delta in the middle, nothing to degenerate.

Where is the cursor? The cursor is the center of its polar sphere. pole = up, refX = horizontal, and the 3rd line is the view pointing into the screen — you look along it. The drag's r places the radius point on the surface in the refX–pole plane; at the first instant r = 0, so center and point coincide; as you drag it opens out to r, and the origin slides by that vector (right figure).

The pan recenters the scene's sphere

The mouse's polar sphere hands us a pan slide: a vector of length r at angle in the refX–pole plane (the screen) — r = hypot(Δx, Δy), angle = atan2(Δy, Δx) from refX. (The 3rd line — the view — isn't involved; the pan lives in the plane facing you.)

Applying the pan is one move: the scene's sphere origin slides by that vector, rebased from C to C′ — a straight slide, the same distance the drag carried.

  • nothing rotates and no camera travels through space — only the origin is rebased;
  • then every node's (r, θ, φ) is recomputed about C′ (the red radius below is the node's new r′);
  • the slide's length is the drag's r, so the recenter distance is the drag.

The camera rides the same pan slide as the center, so its own (r, θ, φ) about the center never changes — the view holds steady while the origin moves under it (right figure).

So the mouse's little polar sphere passes its pan slide straight to the scene's big sphere as the recenter vector — direction and distance in one.

Implemented — this is the editor's pan (replacing the Cartesian one). The gesture is a wheel/trackpad scroll burst:
· Input edge: each event's (Δx, Δy) → (r, angle) at once (deltaToPolar); no raw x/y flows past it.
· Visual: during the burst the mouse's polar sphere is drawn centered on the panned-to location (the start anchor trails), with the pan slide of length r shown in the refX–pole plane. It lingers ~450 ms after the last event, then clears.
· Motion: slide camera + target by r in the refX–pole plane (the (right, up) output edge), then recenter the polar origin (set-origin) — C → C′.

Rotation: drag along a great circle

An empty-space drag rotates the view. You grab the point P₀ under the cursor on the sphere; dragging carries the cursor to P₁.

The view turns by the single great-circle move that carries P₀ → P₁ — the geodesic arc between the two points. The turn amount is that arc (the angle between them about the center).

In polar — P₀ = (θ₀, φ₀), P₁ = (θ₁, φ₁) about the pole. That is all it takes:

The rotation is just carry (θ₀, φ₀) → (θ₁, φ₁) along the great circle through them. The two angle pairs fully specify it — no extra quantity to supply, no axis to name, and no need to compute how far it turned (that arc is only a display readout; the rotation is built and applied straight from the two endpoints).

No axis to pick, and no dome. The rotation is fixed by the two points and the great circle through them — there is no separate yaw/pitch/roll or sign to choose, so the wrong-axis bug can't be expressed. The cursor maps straight onto the sphere's surface (a point on the great circle), not onto a raised dome/hemisphere — so there is no in-dome vs off-dome seam.

Zoom: the view–center–node triangle

When a node is selected it becomes the zoom target. Three points then form a triangle:

  • view — the camera;
  • C — the center of the sphere;
  • node — the selected target, on the sphere.

The hypotenuse — the view → node side — is the zoom line: a dolly slides the camera along it, toward the node (zoom in) or away (zoom out). The right angle sits at C, with the legs the radius C → node and the camera's offset C → view.

In polar, take the node N as the pole. The view V is a radius–direction about it, and the zoom line is that radius:

d = radius of V about N, along the held direction û (the hypotenuse). Zoom is a pure radius change — angles held: d′ = max(d·f, MIN_DIST),   f = 1.01s,   V′ = N + d′·û, where s = the scroll amount (a scalar from the wheel at the input edge).   s > 0 → zoom out; s < 0 → zoom in.

The view·center·node triangle ties d to the scene sphere by the law of cosines about C: d² = ρ² + R² − 2·ρ·R·cos γ, where ρ = radius of V about C, R = radius of N about C (the sphere), and γ = the angle at C between the two radii. The right-angle construction (γ = 90°) collapses it to d² = ρ² + R².

Re-aim: a zoom first re-targets the node nearest the cursor, so the hypotenuse always points at a real node — you zoom toward whatever the mouse is on.

The angle that changes

Zoom holds the camera's direction about the node (pure radius change), so the angle that moves is γ at the center — between C → view and C → node.

  • Zoom in: C → view rotates toward the node, so γ shrinks (→ 0 as the camera reaches the node).
  • Zoom out: C → view swings away, so γ grows.

The right-angle construction above is just the single frame where γ = 90°.

Plan: apply the polar rotate / pan / zoom to the code

Goal: navigation math is polar; the camera stays renderer-native (three.js position + quaternion). Rotate, pan, and zoom become polar operations; Cartesian is confined to the camera's storage and the conversion edges in polar.ts. This removes the round-trip, the pole clamp, and the seam (see the Rotation / pan → recenter / Zoom tabs for the per-op math).

Architecture that actually shipped: The camera is NOT angle-of-record. The camera stays renderer-native — three.js owns it as a position vector + orientation quaternion (the medium). All navigation math is polar; Cartesian is confined to two places only: (a) the camera's storage, and (b) the conversion edges in polar.ts (makeFrame, cameraFrame, toWorld, fromWorld, screenToPolar, deltaToPolar). The navigation handler (interaction-controls.ts) holds only polar quantities. Two grep guards enforce this and are wired into the Stop hook: check-polar-only-nav.sh (no Cartesian rotation math in the handler) and check-no-camera-roundtrip.sh (camera state never reconstructed from a position). The split is: Go owns the diagram (polar (r,θ,φ)), TS owns the camera (Cartesian medium). Where a quaternion is read (in cameraFrame/pan), it is reading the renderer's orientation, not doing Euler-angle math — no gimbal lock.

Phase 0 — Foundation: angle-of-record camera store [SUPERSEDED — not built]

The original plan was to store the camera as { frame, r, θ, φ, roll } in a dedicated camera-polar-store.ts, making angles the source of truth. This was deliberately abandoned. Storing orientation as angles reintroduces the very pole singularity we were eliminating — at a pole, θ is undefined, so the angle-of-record representation breaks there. Instead, the camera stays renderer-native (position + quaternion), and polar-only discipline is enforced by guard scripts rather than by the data structure. camera-polar-store.ts was written and then deleted.

  • 0.1–0.5 (angle-of-record store, forward render hook, load conversion): abandoned — wrong shape; the guard-enforced renderer-native approach replaced all of these.

Phase 3 — Rotation [DONE — motion-driven incremental great circle, not the original frozen-grab spec]

Shipped as motion-driven incremental great-circle rotation, not the original frozen-grab/single-big-arc design. Each pointer-move event rotates by the small arc between the previous frame's cursor point and the current one. The rotation axis comes from θ + 90° (an equatorial direction perpendicular to the motion) — pure angle arithmetic, no cross product of the two cursor points. Applied via arcAxisAngle + rotateAboutAxis in polar.ts. No quaternion in the rotation math; no antipode singularity (consecutive points are only a few pixels apart so they are never antipodal); no flip; free roll rides along; pole-crossing is smooth.

Rejected approaches (don't retry these): Cartesian arcball + Bell/Holroyd edge map (silhouette fold) — built and rejected; frozen-grab single-big-arc (antipode flip at large drags) — built and rejected; turntable (no free roll) — rejected by design.
